# Business Architecture

## 1. Purpose of This Document

This document defines the business architecture for the **Dis-Chem Omni-Channel Inventory Orchestrator**. Its purpose is to show how the proposed platform aligns with strategic priorities, business capabilities, stakeholder needs, value streams, performance measures, and operating model requirements.

In enterprise terms, business architecture provides the bridge between strategic intent and solution design. It ensures that the platform is not framed merely as a technical build, but as a structured response to a high-value business problem.

---

## 2. Strategic Business Context

Dis-Chem is evolving beyond the traditional model of a retail pharmacy chain toward a broader healthcare and wellness platform. This evolution increases operational complexity because inventory now supports not only retail availability, but also chronic medication continuity, clinic-linked operations, regulated dispensing processes, and a more integrated omni-channel customer experience.

As a result, inventory decisions have become more strategically significant. They affect working capital, margin protection, product availability, compliance, and customer trust. The role of inventory management therefore shifts from an operational control activity to an enterprise capability that directly supports strategic performance.

---

## 3. Business Drivers

The platform is shaped by a set of core business drivers.

### 3.1 Financial performance

Inventory is one of the largest controllable balance sheet levers in retail and healthcare-adjacent operations. Excess stock traps working capital, while poor stock availability undermines sales and service levels. The business therefore requires better inventory visibility and faster intervention capability.

### 3.2 Operational efficiency

The organization needs more efficient replenishment, improved store-level inventory balancing, and better visibility across channels. The ability to identify overstock, understock, and unusual inventory patterns is central to operational performance improvement.

### 3.3 Margin protection

Expiry-sensitive stock, especially within pharmacy-related categories, creates direct margin risk. Better inventory intelligence reduces write-offs and improves commercial resilience.

### 3.4 Compliance and governance

Because parts of the inventory landscape involve controlled and compliance-sensitive products, the business requires traceability, auditability, and defensible reporting processes.

### 3.5 Executive decision support

Leadership requires a reliable KPI layer and business-oriented explanations of inventory performance so that action can be taken with speed and confidence.

---

## 4. Strategic Objectives Supported by the Platform

The business architecture aligns the platform to the following strategic objectives:

1. **Optimize working capital**  
   Reduce excess inventory and improve the productivity of stock investment.

2. **Improve inventory efficiency**  
   Reduce inventory days, improve stock turn, and support more accurate replenishment decisions.

3. **Protect margin**  
   Reduce expiry losses, excess carrying costs, and other forms of inventory-related leakage.

4. **Strengthen service reliability**  
   Improve availability of critical products, especially those linked to patient continuity and customer trust.

5. **Improve governance and accountability**  
   Ensure important inventory decisions and compliance-sensitive flows are visible, traceable, and auditable.

6. **Enable data-led leadership**  
   Equip executives with timely, trustworthy, and interpretable performance intelligence.

## 6. Stakeholder Architecture

The value of the platform differs by stakeholder group.

### Executive leadership

Executives need concise visibility into working capital, stock efficiency, risk concentration, and the operational actions requiring intervention.

### Finance and commercial teams

These teams need insight into inventory productivity, margin risk, write-off exposure, and the financial impact of inventory decisions.

### Supply chain and procurement teams

These teams need visibility into replenishment patterns, stock imbalances, high-risk stores, and anticipated demand shifts.

### Pharmacy and clinic operations

These teams need a more reliable view of inventory continuity, regulated movement tracking, and product availability affecting service delivery.

### Data, analytics, and AI teams

These teams need governed datasets, reusable metrics, and traceable logic so that analytical and AI outputs remain consistent and trusted.

### Governance and compliance stakeholders

These stakeholders need transparency, auditability, and evidence that sensitive decision areas are properly controlled.

---

## 7. Value Streams Supported

The platform intersects with several high-value business value streams.

### Procure to stock

Supports decisions on what inventory enters the network, in what quantity, and how efficiently it is distributed.

### Stock to shelf

Improves the conversion of inventory into available products at the point of service and sale.

### Prescription to dispense

Supports continuity, traceability, and visibility for medicine-related service flows.

### Inventory exception to action

Detects inventory risks and converts them into prioritized interventions for managers and executives.

### Insight to decision

Transforms operational data into trusted executive intelligence and action-oriented management information.

---

## 8. Business Information Needs

To function effectively, the business requires a consistent information layer that answers questions such as:

- where is working capital over-concentrated in inventory
- which stores have elevated stock-out or expiry risk
- which product categories show inefficient stock positioning
- how are inventory risks distributed across business units or channels
- which issues require executive escalation versus operational correction
- what trends indicate future replenishment pressure or declining service reliability

The platform is intended to provide these answers in a form that is both analytically robust and executive-friendly.

---

## 9. KPI Architecture

The KPI layer is central to the business architecture because it creates a common language between leadership, finance, operations, and analytics.

### Illustrative KPI domains

- working capital unlocked
- inventory days
- stock turn rate
- expiry exposure
- service risk by store
- controlled dispensing traceability
- chronic patient basket penetration
- reorder point deviation

### Business role of KPIs

These KPIs do more than measure performance. They guide management attention, anchor governance conversations, and define the business success criteria for the platform.

---

## 10. Business Rules and Decision Logic

A credible business architecture must also recognize that decisions are governed by rules, thresholds, and escalation logic.

Examples include:

- thresholds for stock-out and overstock risk
- product handling distinctions between retail and pharmacy categories
- control requirements for compliance-sensitive products
- store or region-specific intervention logic
- escalation rules for high-risk or financially material exceptions

These rules form part of the business logic layer and should be explicitly documented to ensure repeatability and trust.

---

## 11. Operating Model Implications

The platform implies a more mature operating model in which data and analytics are embedded into routine business management.

This means:

- executives review consistent KPI packs rather than fragmented reports
- analysts investigate issues using trusted shared data products
- supply chain teams act on prioritized exceptions rather than intuition alone
- pharmacy and clinic teams gain visibility into service-sensitive stock conditions
- governance stakeholders review controlled audit trails where required

The business architecture therefore supports not only visibility, but also a more disciplined decision-making model.

---

## 12. Business Risks Addressed

The platform is designed to reduce exposure to several business risks:

- excess working capital tied up in slow-moving stock
- stock-outs affecting patient and customer experience
- expiry-related margin erosion
- inconsistent reporting across functions
- delayed reaction to high-risk inventory conditions
- poor traceability in regulated or audit-sensitive contexts

By reducing these risks, the architecture supports both financial and operational resilience.

---

## 13. Business Architecture Summary

The **Dis-Chem Omni-Channel Inventory Orchestrator** should be understood as a strategic business capability, not just a technical solution. It supports financial efficiency, service reliability, governance, and executive decision-making by providing a unified inventory intelligence layer across the enterprise.

This business architecture provides the rationale for the downstream data, application, and technology architecture layers. In other words, it explains why the solution matters before defining how it is implemented.
